Under standard federal privacy regulations, all patient names, direct identifiers, and personal details must be completely removed from all clinical documents, case report forms, and study reports prior to submission to the Food and Drug Administration (FDA). This strict privacy requirement is intended to prevent the unauthorized exposure of personal information during the regulatory review process. Researchers and sponsors must implement rigorous redaction and data-cleaning workflows to strip all direct identifiers, such as names, telephone numbers, and email addresses, substituting them with random alphanumeric subject codes.

Specifically, sensitive records such as social security numbers (SSNs) and health insurance identification numbers are strictly classified as protected health information (PHI) under federal law. The inclusion of these numbers in any submission is considered a serious compliance violation and a significant breach of patient confidentiality. Because these identifiers carry a high risk of identity theft and unauthorized tracking, they must never be included in datasets or clinical narratives shared with the FDA or other public health agencies.

To achieve regulatory compliance, all de-identification of health datasets must strictly follow either the HIPAA Safe Harbor method or the Expert Determination method. The Safe Harbor method requires the removal of 18 specific identifiers, including names, geographic subdivisions smaller than a state, and all elements of dates directly related to an individual. Alternatively, the Expert Determination method requires a qualified statistical expert to apply scientific principles to assess and manage the risk of re-identification, ensuring the risk is extremely small. Both methods are widely recognized as standard practices for maintaining the confidentiality and integrity of patient data.
