Pursuant to the regulations outlined in 21 CFR 201, the established or proprietary product name of a drug must be displayed with prominent visibility and emphasis on the principal display panel (PDP). The presentation must be designed in a clear, legible font and must be placed in a conspicuous position to ensure that consumers, pharmacists, and medical practitioners can easily identify the pharmaceutical product without confusion. The text size, contrast, and layout should prevent any obfuscation from brand logos or graphics.

Furthermore, the net quantity of contents in the package must be explicitly declared on the label. This declaration is required to be expressed in both metric units (such as milliliters or grams) and United States customary units (such as fluid ounces or grains) to facilitate international alignment and ease of understanding for all end-users. The net quantity statement must be printed in a specific area of the principal display panel and adhere to regulatory specifications for minimum height and spacing to prevent misinterpretation of the package volume.

The drug labeling must also include a comprehensive list of all active and inactive ingredients. These ingredients must be listed in a descending order of predominance by weight, ensuring that the substances comprising the largest portion of the formulation are listed first. Any inactive excipients, preservatives, or color additives must follow the active pharmaceutical ingredients in this structured sequence, providing patients and healthcare professionals with critical information regarding potential allergens or sensitivities.

Lastly, the directions for use must be written in plain, clear, and concise language that is easily accessible to a layperson. The instructions should avoid overly dense clinical jargon and instead use simple terms to describe the correct dosage, frequency of administration, and potential contraindications. Providing legible and understandable instructions is essential for minimizing medication errors, promoting patient compliance, and ensuring that non-professional users can administer or consume the therapeutic agent safely and effectively.
