According to the clinical trial regulations enforced by the European Medicines Agency (EMA), the mandatory registration of a clinical trial in a public registry must occur before the enrolment of the very first patient. The trial sponsor is legally obligated to submit all required protocol details, clinical objectives, and investigator information to the designated repository, such as the Clinical Trials Information System (CTIS), prior to initiating any screening or active intervention. This upfront registration ensures public transparency, prevents reporting bias, and fosters trust in clinical research across European Union member states.

To safeguard patient rights and comply with regulatory archives policies, all informed consent documentation obtained from trial participants must be securely retained for a minimum of 25 years post-trial. The retention period begins immediately after the formal conclusion or premature termination of the clinical study. Sponsors and investigational sites are required to maintain these records in a highly secure, temperature-controlled, and restricted-access archive to enable long-term audits, handle potential legal inquiries, and verify that subject consent was properly obtained and documented during the active trial phases.

Pharmacovigilance compliance mandates that any serious adverse events (SAEs) that are life-threatening or result in death must be formally reported to the competent authority within 7 days of the sponsor first becoming aware of the event. Follow-up reports containing detailed medical assessments and patient status must be submitted within an additional 8 days. Non-fatal or non-life-threatening serious adverse events are subject to a 15-day reporting window. These strict timelines ensure that regulatory bodies can continuously assess the safety profile of the investigational agent and intervene immediately if patient safety is compromised.

Finally, every investigational medicinal product (IMP) distributed to clinical sites must be clearly and securely labelled with its unique batch number and explicit expiry date. This labeling is crucial for maintaining complete traceability throughout the drug supply chain, facilitating immediate product recalls if safety issues arise, and preventing the administration of expired therapeutic agents. The label must also include a statement identifying the product as an investigational drug, the name of the sponsor, and the protocol code, all visible on the primary packaging to prevent any mix-ups during clinical trials.
